What is StakeStone?

StakeStone is a crypto-native financial infrastructure protocol focused on yield-bearing assets, omnichain liquidity and programmable accounts. Its public materials describe a stack for self-custodied accounts, onchain settlement, automated yield routes and agent-ready payments. The official StakeStone website is the primary starting point for current product access, documentation and app links.

How StakeStone works

StakeStone routes different asset types through different yield and settlement paths. ETH, BTC-linked assets and stablecoins should be reviewed separately.

  1. Choose an asset routeETH users evaluate STONE, BTC users evaluate SBTC or STONEBTC, and stablecoin users evaluate STONEUSD where available.
  2. Mint a yield tokenThe protocol mints a token that represents a share of the relevant asset pool, with exchange-rate accounting used for deposits and redemptions.
  3. Use omnichain liquiditySTONE and related assets may be bridged or used in DeFi integrations, but liquidity, route support and market pricing can vary by chain.
  4. Exit through the shown routeWithdrawals depend on the product, buffer liquidity, settlement timing, smart contracts and any custody or strategy layer involved.

Tokens and yield routes

The main StakeStone products are not interchangeable. Each token has its own backing model, accounting method and risk profile.

STONE for ETH

STONE is described in the docs as a non-rebase ERC-20 for ETH yield, where wallet balance does not grow but the contract exchange rate can change. Read the official What is STONE page before comparing it with ordinary ETH or liquid staking tokens.

STONEUSD and STONEBTC

StakeStone's Yield Layer says stablecoin and BTC products use smart contracts plus custodial and execution infrastructure, unlike STONE's onchain ETH staking route. The Yield Layer documentation explains the distinction.

STO governance

STO is the governance token for StakeStone's omnichain liquidity infrastructure, with veSTO mechanics, voting and incentive allocation. Treat STO exposure as a separate token decision, not a requirement for holding every StakeStone yield token.

Fees, security and withdrawals

StakeStone costs and exits are route-dependent. Review the live app flow and official docs before signing a transaction.

Cost components

Costs may include network gas, wallet approval gas, protocol route components, swap spread, market slippage, bridge messaging and product-specific settlement costs. Do not assume a fixed fee.

Withdrawal paths

For STONE, StakeStone documents request and buffer-based withdrawal paths, with the faster buffer route depending on available liquidity. The official withdrawal guide explains the route and exchange-rate logic.

Risk disclosures

StakeStone's own risk page names smart-contract, third-party protocol, underlying asset, rebalancing, cross-chain, external-use and regulatory risks. Audits help with review but do not remove protocol risk.

StakeStone FAQ

Is StakeStone the official StakeStone app?

No. This is an independent guide to StakeStone, not the official app, wallet or support channel. Use the official StakeStone website or docs when you need current app links, product terms, contract addresses or support resources.

What is STONE in StakeStone?

STONE is StakeStone's ETH-linked yield token. The official docs describe it as a non-rebase ERC-20, meaning the wallet balance does not increase automatically while the contract exchange rate reflects accumulated yield mechanics. Read What is STONE before using it.

How does StakeStone generate yield?

It depends on the asset route. StakeStone's Yield Layer says ETH yield is generated onchain through staking and restaking protocols, while BTC and stablecoin products can involve smart contracts, custodial infrastructure and execution strategies. See the Yield Layer docs.

Can I withdraw from StakeStone at any time?

You should not assume every route exits the same way. STONE has request and buffer-based withdrawal paths, and the buffer route depends on available liquidity. BTC and stablecoin products may use different settlement processes. Start with the official withdrawal documentation.

Does StakeStone have smart-contract risk?

Yes. StakeStone's risk disclosure lists smart-contract risk, DeFi composability risk, underlying asset risk, allocation risk, cross-chain risk and regulatory risk. Its audit page is useful for scope review, but audits do not remove risk. Read the official risk page.

Where can I verify StakeStone token addresses?

Use the official contract address pages before adding a token, bridging or signing an approval. For STONE, StakeStone lists mainnet and chain-specific addresses in its Smart Contracts Address_STONE documentation.
